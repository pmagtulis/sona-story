By Prinz Magtulis

Actions speak louder than words, or so the cliche goes, but that is not the case when you are president when everything you say carries the weight of power and authority.
The Philippine leader best exemplifies this through the annual State of the Nation Address (SONA). Every fourth Monday of July, the constitution mandates that presidents give a SONA to open a new session of Congress. It has become a yearly spectacle: the House of Representatives, where the speech is delivered, gets a face-lift, while lawmakers and guests in their formal attire walk on a red carpet. In recent years, film directors are even hired to ensure the event runs smoothly.
But all the glamor fades into the background once the president stands behind the lectern. There, they command the room with just their words, lots of them. An analysis of the SONA since the second Philippine Republic showed that the president's annual speech runs an average of over 7,600 words, which takes approximately about an hour to deliver, heavily interrupted by applause from a live audience.
The SONA has no time limit, so some speak more than the others: Rodrigo Duterte holds the record of having the longest SONA among all Philippine leaders post-dictatorship. The current president, Ferdinand “Bongbong” Marcos Jr., delivered his first 7,990-word SONA in an hour and 10 minutes in 2022.

But speech length is hardly the point of the SONA. Charles Ladia, a professor who studies political communication at University of the Philippines, said some words carry more weight than others. Context is important as the SONA, like any other speeches, are delivered with a political objective, which can be anything from pleading legislators for support to their plans and agenda, diffusing tensions, or solidifying or recovering a waning political capital halfway through their six-year term.
"It is, of course, an agenda-setting speech," Manuel Quezon III, a historian, said. "So it must inform and then muster support."
Under the charter, the president is mandated within 30 days to submit to Congress his proposed budget for the following fiscal year. Quezon said this makes the SONA a “sales pitch” of the president to legislators, the first chance for the chief executive to sell his programs for public funding.
Throughout the years, the pitch generally looked and sounded the same. SONA, after all, is historically a formal event and that dictates the language it takes, Ladia said. Presidents typically used English to address not only lawmakers, but other guests, including foreign dignitaries. By count, words like "government," "people,"" "program," and "development" were most commonly used. "Congress", to whom or where he addresses his message to, also appeared frequently in the text.

That said, how these words were used in the SONA varied in context. For instance, our analysis showed "government" either referred to present or past administrations mentioned in a speech. A study, meanwhile, said that frequent use of “development” implied that speeches “focused on the growth of the country" ("economic" is also often used), while "people" meant SONA aimed to address the people's needs.
But the same words could also be so generic, and their frequent mention hardly inferred anything relevant, in some cases. Our analysis then ran an algorithm that counterbalanced the frequency of word usage against the number of speeches- in this case, 83– where these words appear. This method is known as a combination of term frequency and inverse document frequency (TF-IDF).

Our analysis showed a starkly different results than when just counting words: while common words like "government" were still found relevant from time to time, more specific words, which carried deeper meanings, and were considered much relevant to the speeches.

Take Gloria Arroyo's SONA in 2002. She mentioned the words "strong republic" in her 4,519-word speech in a bid to portray government stability amid an economic and fiscal crisis that came after a people's uprising that toppled her predecessor, Joseph Estrada.

"Strong Republic" eventually became her government's political slogan, found emblazoned in government-funded projects.

Arroyo was not alone in using her SONA to build a political agenda. The late Benigno Aquino III had “Kayo ang boss ko (You are my bosses)” in 2010, pertaining to Filipinos and how he was accountable to them. “Wang-wang” also became an image of his anti-corruption platform. A Filipino slang for a vehicle siren, Aquino likened its use on the road to government officials using their position to take advantage of the public.
Fidel Ramos also had "Philippines 2000" in 1993, which also acted as his main economic program. In addition, Arroyo used her SONA to justify controversial policies such as the Expanded Value-Added Tax (E-VAT) law, while Duterte repeatedly defended his drug war.
Mostly though, Quezon said Arroyo's SONA were largely for survival. "They pruned down her SONAs as the main point of them was not their contents, but her ability to survive to deliver them at all," he said. Arroyo delivered the shortest SONA in history in 2005, and "critics" were among the more relevant words in her last SONA in 2009– a signal, Ladia said, that Arroyo recognized the opposition that she faced at the time.
In general however, a president's last SONA is reserved to highlight their so-called "legacy" and therefore, tended to be the longest among most presidents, our analysis showed.
SONA were also heavily dictated by the biggest challenges an administration faced at the time. "SARS" or the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome for Arroyo in 2003, "Angelo" in 2004 when Angelo de la Cruz, an overseas Filipino worker was held and freed in Afghanistan. There were also "Covid" and the "pandemic" for Duterte.

At the same time, the SONA served as a venue to attack. Estrada waged an all-out war against the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF) in Mindanao in 2000– both “MILF” and “Mindanao” were among the more relevant words found in his speech then. Arroyo criticized the Manila Electric Co., while Aquino lambasted the Metropolitan Waterworks and Sewerage System (MWSS) for excessive bonuses.
"I think that this is very reflective of his populist politics— that he alone as the absolute representation of popular will sets and decides on the direction of governance in the country," Cleve Arguelles, political scientist at De La Salle University, said. "The imagined relationship between Duterte and Filipinos is informal, personal and even unmediated."

When words matter

These themes do not just accidentally appear in the SONA. Months before the president delivers an address, the staff gather inputs from various agencies for the speech. There are speechwriters who collate them and do the drafts, but presidents obviously have the final say. In the end, not all are mentioned in the SONA. In Duterte's case, a big chunk of it was disregarded in the speech proper.
Duterte was fond of giving off-the-cuff remarks during his SONA, but when he did, he uttered words that ultimately dictated the theme of his speech – and policy. He used the word "oligarchy" to attack broadcaster ABS-CBN Corp. which lost its broadcasting franchise two months before the SONA, and was one of the only three presidents so far that mentioned "elite", apart from Joseph Estrada and Marcos Sr.
In the middle of a speech, he also sometimes “code switched” to Bisaya, the language in his hometown Davao. When switching to Filipino, he also typically used "ko" or "ako" (me) or "kayo" and "ninyo" (you). This was a departure from Aquino, the only president to deliver the SONA mainly in Filipino, who involved the audience in his speech with "tayo"(us), "natin", and "atin" (ours).
"I think that this is very reflective of his populist politics— that he alone as the absolute representation of popular will sets and decides on the direction of governance in the country," Cleve Arguelles, political scientist at De La Salle University, said. "The imagined relationship between Duterte and Filipinos is informal, personal and even unmediated."
